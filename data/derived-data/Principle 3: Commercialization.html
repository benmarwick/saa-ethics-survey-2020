33 CRM, in particular for-profit archaeological consulting, by its very nature commercializes archaeological objects under the guise of scholarship and legal compliance. Increasingly, it seems that the commercial value of some archaeological sites and objects are increasing well beyond their scholastic or cultural value. Two examples: 1) hundreds to thousands of dollars spent on documenting barely-50-year-old trash concentrations during a single inventory project; 2) tens of thousands of dollars to "mitigate" a small sliver of an archaeological site containing one previously documented grinding slick and a few dozen ceramic sherds and flakes (most of which had been previously analyzed).

37 This is woefully inadequate and incomplete. We take a strong ethical position about the commercialization of objects but say nothing about commercialization of the data, knowledge and intellectual outcomes of our work. We recognize there are lots of negative externalities to the commercial exploitation of physical remains. The ethical problems of draconian copyright laws and the intrusive surveillance of commercial digital publishing platforms (not to mention social media platforms) get very little attention and discussion in the SAA. These are big problems. If we are serious about making archaeology more inclusive and democratic, then we need to consider the ethics of how we communicate archaeology.

This provision was included in ARPA to combat the unauthorized trafficking of archaeological resources. 287 I agree with the ideas and sentiments in this principle, but the reality is that the commercial market is out of our control. I have long had to deal with this due to the location of much of my research. My own approach is to let the dealers know that I oppose their activities and I explain why in as much detail as possible. But I still try to treat them in a civil manner and encourage them to share information. In most cases, this has resulted in saving information that otherwise would have been lost forever. I periodically send this information to relevant site files or other repositories to ensure that the information is part of the official record. I don't know if the wording of the principle could be altered to incorporate this aspect, but I consider it a facet of public archaeology that is a sort of last-ditch effort to save as much archaeological information as possible. So many times, once the original finder or dealer sells the item, all of the original provenience information is lost. If it changes hands several times over the years, all it takes is one temporary owner to discard all of the associated information and it is gone forever. Even worse is when one of these people attributes the item to a different site to increase its value. I am dealing with a situation of this type at present. So I think there should be some encouragement to try and salvage any information possible. Perhaps it doesn't belong in these principles--I'll let the committee decide that.

1318 I would note that there is a challenge to acting on this principle in that it is often the case that important sites/collections are found and owned by private individuals. Such sites and collections can include important archaeological information that, if not researched, will be lost to the profession. It is true that by doing such research archaeologists may provide information that "enhances the commercial value" of a collection. However, since the development of the ethics principles in 1996, the internet has created and sustained a large commercial marketplace, so it's not necessarily the case that archaeological research contributes to the enhancement of commercial value. I believe that our challenge is to adhere to Principle 1, Stewardship, while doing our best to inform private individuals about what we see as the importance of sharing information about their objects with the profession and the public, while not contributing to the commercialization of archaeological objects or sites. Discussions with these individuals about donating their collections and records to public repositories can produce the desired outcome. However, for some parts of the archaeological record in North America, amateurs/private citizens find and hold the lion's share of sites and objects. To ignore this is, in my opinion, short-sighted and impoverishes our knowledge of the past. I would prefer to see this principle rewritten to take a more nuanced stance on working with privately-held collections while retaining the language about not inadvertently contributing to the commercialization of archaeological objects.
